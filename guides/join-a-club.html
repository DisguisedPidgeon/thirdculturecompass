The "Join a Club" Advice Doesn't Work If You Don't Belong Yet

5 min read Written from experience

At some point — probably within the first two weeks — a counselor, a teacher, or a well-meaning adult is going to tell you to join a club or try out for a sport. They'll say it like it's simple. Like belonging is a door you just have to walk through.

What they don't tell you is that the groups in those clubs formed months or years before you arrived. The friendships are already built. The inside jokes already exist. The social architecture of your new school was constructed without you in it, and nobody left a space.

This isn't anyone's fault. It's just how it works. And the "join a club" advice, while technically correct, skips over the hardest part: you can be in the room and still not belong in it.

The reset is real

Here's something that doesn't get said enough: every time you move, you don't just lose your friend group. You lose the version of yourself that existed there. At your old school, people knew your humor, your habits, how you acted when you were comfortable. They'd seen you be good at things. They had context for who you were.

At the new school, none of that transfers. You start from zero — not just socially, but in terms of how people perceive you. The confident, funny, capable version of you that took years to build somewhere else has to be rebuilt from scratch. That takes time. More time than anyone's willing to officially acknowledge.

After enough resets, you don't stop being able to make friends. You just get numb to starting over. You know how to begin. You also know how it ends when you move again. That numbness is real, and nobody warned me about it.

What actually helps

Not "join a club." Not "put yourself out there." More specific than that.

Find one person first, not a group

Groups are hard to enter cold. One person is easier. In a new class, in a lunch line, in a hallway — one conversation with one person is more useful than trying to insert yourself into an established friend group. If that one person is decent, they'll introduce you to others. If they don't, you find the next one. One at a time is slower but it actually works.

Use the awkward first week strategically

The first week is actually the easiest time to ask questions that would feel weird later. "What class is this?" "Where's the gym?" "Do you know if this teacher is strict?" People expect new students to not know things — use that window. It's a natural conversation starter that closes after about two weeks.

Let yourself follow someone

This sounds embarrassing but it isn't. If someone in your first period is clearly also going to your second period, follow them. Ask if you can walk together. It's not weird — it's practical. Schools are confusing. New schedules are confusing. A lot of people remember being new and will help if you just ask directly.

Stop performing okayness

New students often feel pressure to seem like they're fine — like the transition is easy, like they're not lost, like nothing is hard. That performance is exhausting and it keeps people at a distance. You don't have to broadcast that you're struggling. But you don't have to pretend you're completely fine either. A small amount of honesty — "I moved here a month ago and I'm still figuring it out" — tends to open more doors than acting like you've always been there.

On the numbness

If you've moved multiple times, you might notice that making friends gets easier in some ways and harder in others. Easier because you know the mechanics — you know how to introduce yourself, how to find the right people, how to navigate the first month. Harder because you also know that it might end. Another move, another reset, another round of starting from zero.

That awareness can make you hold back. Why invest in something that might end? Why let people know you well if you're just going to leave?

There's no clean answer to that. But the numbness is worth naming — because it's not the same as not caring. It's a kind of self-protection that makes sense given what you've been through. Recognizing it is at least a start.

For the counselors reading this

If a student tells you they're having trouble connecting socially, "join a club" is not enough. Ask them what their previous schools were like. Ask how many times they've moved. Ask what they miss. The students who've moved the most are often the most practiced at seeming fine. They're not always fine.

Actually useful first-week advice
  • Find one person in each class whose name you learn on day one
  • Ask questions during the first week — the window closes fast
  • Let yourself follow someone between classes if you're lost
  • Don't perform okayness — small honesty opens more doors
  • Give it longer than you think it should take — belonging isn't fast

You're not behind. You're just new. Those are different things.
